# Research: Idea 1 Relationship Trust-Path Mapper

Research date: 2026-06-27

Method:

- Online research only.
- Parallel subagent research focused on mid-career working professionals.
- No user interviews yet.
- Goal: identify JTBD patterns, user-journey pain, willingness-to-pay moments, alternatives, and MVP wedge.

## Executive summary

Idea 1 is strongest when a mid-career professional is actively trying to access a high-value opportunity and cold applications feel broken.

The monetizable wedge is not "networking" or "career coaching"; it is a **Trust Path Sprint** before applying cold.

Core promise:

> Before you apply cold, find the highest-trust path into this role or company.

## Target segment

Primary target:

- Mid-career and senior working professionals.
- 5-20 years of experience.
- Strongest beachhead: 7-15 years of experience.
- Likely age range: 28-45.
- Strongest age range: 30-42.
- Functions: tech, finance, consulting, product, engineering, AI, sustainability, operations, strategy, and GTM.

Why this segment:

- They have enough former employers, colleagues, vendors, clients, alumni networks, communities, and projects to create real trust paths.
- They have enough income to pay for career leverage.
- One successful opportunity can be worth tens of thousands of dollars in salary, equity, consulting, or career upside.
- They know cold applications are noisy, but they often underuse their weak-tie network.

## Core JTBD

When I find a high-value career opportunity but I do not have obvious access, help me map the fastest credible trust path through my real career history and relationships, so I can make a warm and contextual move instead of relying on cold applications or generic outreach.

## Journey map

| Stage | Struggling moment | Pain intensity | Willingness to pay | Product implication |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| Passive career monitoring | "I know many people, but I do not know who is useful now." | Medium | Low | Useful retention feature, weak acquisition hook. |

## Where the pain is

Highest pain appears when the user has a specific target but no trusted route in.

Examples:

- I want to reach a hiring manager at this company.
- I want a warm referral before applying.
- I want to switch function or industry and need borrowed credibility.
- I have hundreds of LinkedIn contacts, but I do not know who can actually help.
- I am worried my resume is being lost in AI-filtered application volume.

## Where they are willing to pay

Willingness to pay is highest when:

- The user is actively searching for 30-90 days.
- The user is employed but discreetly exploring.
- The user is targeting senior or high-compensation roles.
- The user is switching industry/function and needs credibility.
- The user has 500+ contacts but no relationship system.
- The user believes a referral materially improves their odds.
- The cost of failure is high: unemployment, stagnation, or missing a major career jump.

Weak willingness-to-pay moments:

- Passive networking.
- General career exploration.
- Junior jobseekers with thin networks.
- Users who only want resume polish.

## Existing alternatives

| Alternative | What it proves | Why it fails for this JTBD |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn Premium | Professionals already pay for job search and networking leverage. | It gives visibility, InMail, and insights, but not user-specific trust-path reasoning. |
| Teal / Huntr | Users pay for job search workflow and resume/application management. | Relationship path is secondary. |
| Mesh / Dex / Covve / folk | Users pay to manage professional contacts. | Generic CRM does not start from a target opportunity. |
| Notion / Airtable / spreadsheets | Users can manually track contacts and applications. | Data decays quickly and requires discipline. |
| Career coaches | Users pay for high-touch career leverage. | Expensive, not scalable, and not network-aware by default. |
| Alumni groups / Slack / WhatsApp / Discord | Communities carry trust. | Weak memory, weak workflow, poor follow-through tracking. |
| Blind/referral swaps | People seek referrals when desperate. | Often low-trust, low-context, and reputation-risky. |

## MVP wedge

Build a **Trust Path Sprint**.

MVP flow:

1. User uploads resume or LinkedIn-style profile text.
2. User imports contacts manually or through CSV.
3. User pastes 5-20 target companies, people, or job links.
4. Product maps first-degree and second-degree trust paths.
5. Each path gets a practical score:
   - relationship strength
   - recency
   - credibility
   - company proximity
   - role relevance
   - intro friction
   - proof-of-work fit
6. Output: top people to contact this week, why them, what to ask, and follow-up timing.
7. Track outcomes:
   - advice call
   - referral submitted
   - hiring manager intro
   - no response
   - dead end

## Pricing hypothesis

- Free: 1 target company, limited path map.
- Active Search: US$29-US$49/month for 10-25 target companies, outreach workflow, and follow-up tracking.
- Sprint Pack: US$99-US$199 for a 30-day trust-path search sprint.
- Maintenance Plan: US$10-US$15/month for passive relationship monitoring.
- Later B2B: outplacement firms, MBA/alumni groups, bootcamps, professional associations.

Best first test:

> Concierge Trust Path Sprint for 10 users. Manually produce trust maps, simulate or charge US$99, then measure whether users actually contact people and get replies/referrals/interviews.

## Key risks

- Becomes "another CRM" instead of target-specific opportunity access.
- Overpromises referrals; referrals improve odds but do not replace role fit.
- LinkedIn/Gmail data access risk.
- Creepy if it scores people too aggressively.
- Generic AI outreach can damage reputation.
- Network-based hiring can amplify bias; product should emphasize consent, fit, and context.
